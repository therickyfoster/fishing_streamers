Core Receptive Layers

Think of this as a simple stack you can remix anywhere you show up: personal life, online spaces, local projects, or planetary-scale experiments. Each layer makes the next one safer and more powerful.

1. Inner Layer: Psychological Safety
No Punishment for Showing Up
We start by protecting the nervous system. A receptive node is a place where people won’t be mocked, punished, or exiled for saying the quiet part out loud. That includes saying “no”, asking “why?”, and admitting “I’m not ok”.
  • Normalize “I might be wrong, but here’s what I see.”
  • Leaders go first in admitting mistakes; that sets the ceiling on honesty.
  • Replace humiliation with curiosity when something breaks.
2. Circle Layer: Mutual Aid & Reciprocity
Solidarity, Not Charity
Receptive spaces run on exchange, not extraction. Mutual aid means we move resources, time, and information in ways that keep dignity intact and power circulating, not hoarded.
  • Everyone can be a giver and a receiver at different times.
  • We design flows so that care doesn’t burn out the same 3 people.
  • We name and celebrate invisible work (emotional labour, translation, holding the group together).
3. Mesh Layer: Regenerative Designs
More Life In, Not Less
A receptive node doesn’t just avoid harm, it actively restores. Regenerative design asks: after we work together, is there more health in the land, the people, and the relationships?
  • Food forests, community gardens, and shared tools instead of scarcity games.
  • Systems that cycle resources locally and reduce dependency on fragile centralized supply chains.
  • Projects that make it easier for the next person to do something kind and courageous.
4. Governance Layer: Transparent & Trauma-Informed
Nothing About Us Without Us
Receptive spaces are explicit about how decisions are made. We assume people are carrying visible and invisible trauma, so we don’t design processes that casually re-wound them.
  • Clear, written agreements instead of vibes-only expectations.
  • Resident / member voice in decisions that affect them; no secret deals in back rooms.
  • Rhythms for repair: when harm happens, we have a way back, not just a way out.
5. Signal Layer: Story, Art & Invitations
Broadcasting Safety
People who hid for safety watch for consistency over time. They notice who gets protected, who gets thrown under the bus, and who gets quietly erased. So we broadcast differently.
  • We tell stories that centre care, not conquest.
  • We make explicit invitations to those who were silenced first and worst.
  • We say: “You can enter slowly, leave anytime, and choose your level of visibility.”
